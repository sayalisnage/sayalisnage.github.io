Low-Voltage Level Power System Modelling

Forecasting energy consumption and maximum demand in residential and small commercial systems using time series data from Southern Germany.

View on GitHub

Project Overview

This project focuses on forecasting energy consumption and maximum demand at the low-voltage level using time series data collected from 11 households in Southern Germany between 2014 and 2020. It analyzes 15- and 60-minute interval data covering overall energy consumption, photovoltaic generation, electric vehicle charging, and appliance-level usage. The aim is to identify load patterns and build regression models that predict peak electricity demand to support energy efficiency and grid reliability.

Data Sources

  • Open Power System Data: Time series data from 11 households including total consumption, PV generation, EV charging, and appliance-level loads at 15- and 60-minute intervals.
    https://open-power-system-data.org/

Methodology

  1. Data Preparation and Cleaning
    • Standardized column names and removed duplicates.
    • Filtered data to December 2015 through June 2017.
    • Aggregated and imputed missing values for PV generation and demand.
  2. Feature Engineering & Dimensionality Reduction
    • Correlation analysis to identify key features for maximum demand.
    • Applied PCA to reduce dimensionality while retaining 90% variance.
    • Split data into training and test sets (80/20).
  3. Model Training & Evaluation
    • Trained multiple regression models including Linear, Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net, KNN, Decision Tree, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting.
    • Evaluated performance using R², MAE, and RMSE metrics.

Results Summary

  • Gradient Boosting, K-Nearest Neighbors, and Random Forest models achieved R² ≈ 0.99, indicating strong predictive accuracy.
  • PCA improved model robustness by reducing overfitting in simpler models.
  • Appliance-level consumption showed strong correlation with maximum demand.

Limitations

  • Geographic scope limited to Southern Germany; may affect generalization.
  • Weather, appliance specs, and tariff effects were not fully modeled.
  • Unforeseen events such as load shedding introduce uncertainty.

Future Work

  • Integrate weather and tariff data for enhanced forecasting.
  • Develop real-time monitoring dashboards.
  • Expand to other regions and larger datasets.
  • Explore deep learning models for sequence forecasting.
